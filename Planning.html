Applications

This project whilst being simple has many practical applications most notably in agriculture where it could be deployed to monitor humidity levels and other temperature information which would assist farmers in keeping track of their crops.

Other cases where it could be useful is for specialised machinery or instruments that must be kept at a constant temperature. This chatbot would be able to quickly alert the user if any temperatures changed. 

Motivation

The motivation behind this simple project is education. Although the project is simple enough for a team to build within a reasonable amount of time, the goal of this project is to educate high school children and get them excited about information technology and software/hardware engineering.

The temperature and humidity monitor and the chatbot are interactive and easy for high school children to build over a few sessions which will allow them to gain an appreciation for technology and help guide the interested children to the science and technology pathway.

With this project we hope to expose the high school children to minor coding, configuring small electrical components and make it a team building activity.

Aims

Our project concerns about climate changes have severe impact on our planet. Global warming has been heavily damaged to Australian disasters like bushfires, torrential rain, and the Australian economy.

By bushfire alone, billions of wildlife animals have been taken, and unfortunately some of Australian firefighter volunteers have fatality accidents while on mission, except for the breakout of coronavirus maybe part of our planet event.

Our solutions raise the awareness of climate change, and dedicate potential tools engineered high-end technology to enhance the education programs, student activities to outreach on climate change, as well as the possibly accuracy prediction for any coming natural disaster based on our experimental data.

Testing

After we finished building a mock of the temperature and humidity sensor using the arduino kit and then trying to incorporate that with the ChatBot we moved on to the testing phase. In the testing phase we started with some basic tests like does the arduino unit work and do what it is supposed to do.

The chatbot was a bit more dynamic, the system runs of python programming language and needs vast pool of questions before it is fully operational but for the time being we have a proof of concept. This was tested the same way: does the chatbot load, then does it display answers to questions posed by the users. 

Once the major problems such as issues of the systems not working were ironed out then we moved to the stage where the smaller, detailed problems were tackled such as their redundancies when users poses questions which are not within the data pool. Or when the temperature and humidity exceed the parameters of the sensors. 

Next stage was what happens if problems arise which once the users possess the prototype and will there be a quick troubleshooting guide which will make it easier for the user to troubleshoot the problem.

So we moved on to thinking about the simple problems that occur with electronic items like why isn’t the sensor showing the data - possible answer can be is the unit turned on or does the battery have enough charge?

Once we completed the alpha stage testing, we moved to the beta stage where members of the public (usually just friends) were invited to try out the system.

They found some issues like the system doesn’t respond for 30 seconds after the unit is turned on. We are still working on that. With the chatbot, the major feedback we got was that there needs to be a lot more questions but that can only be done with experts in meteorology and user interface designers who understand the needs of the public when it comes to the questions about weather.

Note: We never had the opportunity to test the full system together rather the systems were tested individually as they are located in different parts of the country. The final testing can only be done with the full assembly.

Risks

Our team project is using the chatbot platform to interact with users for communication so there are some potential risks to the software which creates chatbot.

Chatbot is just simply a software solution that uses machine learning to have a conversation with users. For cybersecurity, there are very common risks including Threats and Vulnerabilities.

Threats

A system can be compromised as Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, Elevation of Privileges.

Types Description of attacks
Spoofing Unlawful access and use another’s credentials. Impersonating or someone else.
Tampering Malicious/malfunction changes, data is modified, wrong data
Repudiation Illegal operation in system
Information Disclosure Stolen data
Denial of Service Users are denied access due to large volumes of traffic been sent by an individual or group.


Attackers can threaten to expose sensitive information (as profiles, credit card details…) or sell personal information on the deep web.

Vulnerabilities

A system will be vulnerable when it is not properly mitigated, easily is attacked by poor maintenance, improper coding, low level of protection, and technical errors. 

Risk to use Arduino system

Arduino kits, humidity sensors, LCD display.

Significant hazards to Arduino equipment: Electrical shock, electric fires, components overheat may cause skin burns.

Risks of humidity sensors: malfunction, damages, inaccuracy, poor quality cause misinformation, poor durability

Risks of LCD displays: broken/damages by impacts, glass fractures.
